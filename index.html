Why I Teach Korean

I built something

I built everything from scratch — tracking systems for every student, personalized study plans, follow-up protocols. Word spread. Within four years, I was ranked 170th out of 450,000 tutors on Korea's largest education platform. The top 0.03%.

Students didn't just pass exams. They started believing in themselves. That was the part I loved most.

Chapter 2 — The Price

Nobody tells you
what it costs.

Korean parents want results. Grades, rankings, certificates — in one of the most competitive education markets in the world, that's what they pay for. And I delivered.

But I also saw kids being crushed by the pressure. So I became more than a teacher. I became a connector — mediating between parents who wanted scores and students who needed someone to just listen. Counselor, study coach, translator between two generations who saw education completely differently.

I took less pay to spend more hours. I hired staff. Office rent, materials, custom curricula — all out of my own pocket. The work was meaningful. The math was not.

"Some days I was a teacher. Other days, a counselor. Always, the person who cared too much to say no."

Chapter 3 — The Breaking Point

I remembered
too well.

I didn't burn out because I stopped caring. I burned out because I couldn't stop caring. Every student's struggle felt personal. Every parent's frustration landed on me. Every improvement felt like proof that if I just worked a little harder, I could fix the whole system.

I couldn't. My body broke before my motivation did. I closed the center. Packed a bag. Left Korea with no plan and no idea what came next.

Sometimes walking away is the hardest thing a teacher does.

Chapter 4 — The Wandering

No plan.
Just breathing.

I traveled. Vietnam, Thailand, different cities, different lives. No schedule for the first time in years. I slept when I was tired. Ate when I was hungry. Talked to strangers without an agenda.

It wasn't a grand adventure. It was recovery. Slow, quiet, sometimes boring. But I needed every minute of it to remember who I was outside of a classroom.

And then something unexpected started happening.

I'd explain, and they'd lean in. I'd demonstrate Korean sounds, and they'd try. I'd connect language to culture, and their eyes would light up. One night, teaching a group of travelers basic Korean phrases over dinner, I realized I was doing the thing I loved again — without even trying.

Teaching Philosophy

Three Pillars of Korean Mastery

These aren't just teaching methods. They're the foundation of how Korean actually works — and why most textbooks get it wrong.

Pronunciation First

Most teachers start with grammar. I start with pronunciation. Even with just 10 words, if you pronounce them correctly, Koreans will think you lived in Korea. Korean is forgiving on grammar but unforgiving on sounds.

CONSONANTS: THREE LEVELS

English has nothing like this. Same letter shape, three completely different sounds.

가

Plain (예사소리)

Soft, relaxed — like "g" in "go" but gentler

카

Aspirated (거센소리)

Strong burst of air — hold your hand in front

까

Tensed (된소리)

Tight throat, no air — like the "k" in "sky"

Confuse these, and "coffee" (커피) becomes "nose bleed" (코피). Seriously.

VOWELS: JAW & LIP POSITIONS

I teach you to feel each vowel physically. No guessing — your jaw and lips tell me exactly what you're saying.

아

Open wide

Drop your jaw as far as it goes — like saying "ah" at the doctor

어

Jaw halfway

Between 아 and 오 — relax your jaw, like the "u" in "fun"

오

Round your lips

Tiny circle with your lips — like blowing out a candle

우

Push lips forward

Like 오 but tighter — say "oo" in "food" with pursed lips

Just by watching your jaw and lips, I can tell exactly which vowel you're trying to say — and where it's going wrong.

NATURAL SPEECH: SOUND BLENDING

Textbooks teach crisp pronunciation. Real Koreans blend sounds together. I teach you both.

맛있어요

Textbook

→

마시셔요

Natural

Sounds merge and flow. I teach you to lean into natural blending, not fight it.

Sentence Logic

Don't memorize patterns. Understand why. English goes small→big. Korean goes big→small. English leads with action. Korean saves the verb for last. Once you see the logic, you stop translating and start thinking in Korean.

WORD ORDER: SHOPPING

Same sentence, two completely different ways of thinking.

English: Who → Action → What → Where

I bought a bag in Paris

Korean: Who → Where → What → Action

저는 파리에서 가방을 샤요

TENSE: JUST THE ENDING CHANGES

In Korean, tense lives at the end. Everything else stays exactly the same.

사요 → buy (present)
샬어요 → bought (past)
살 거예요 → will buy (future)

Learn one pattern → apply it to every verb. That's the power of Korean logic.

BIG → SMALL: ADDRESSES & DATES

The big-to-small rule applies to everything — not just sentences.

English address: 42 Oak Street, Seoul, Korea

Korean address: 한국, 서울, 오크 스트릿 42

English date: February 13, 2026

Korean date: 2026년 2월 13일 (Year → Month → Day)

Koreans think from the biggest context down to the smallest detail. Once you see it, you can't unsee it.

Cultural Context

Korean isn't just a language — it's a social operating system. The same sentence can be polite, casual, or insulting based on context. I don't just teach words — I teach the cultural forces that shaped them.

FORMALITY: 7 LEVELS OF "THANK YOU"

Use the wrong level, and you'll insult someone while trying to be polite.

고마워Close friends only
고마워요Casual polite
감사해요Polite
감사합니다Formal
감사드립니다Very formal / Business

WHY KOREANS ASK YOUR AGE

It's not nosiness — it's a linguistic necessity.

Korean has different verb endings, vocabulary, and even pronouns based on the age relationship. Without knowing your age, a Korean speaker literally doesn't know which words to use. It's like asking "should I use 'you' or 'thou'?" but for every sentence.

밥 먹었어?

To younger

vs

식사 하셨습니까?

To elder

Both mean "Have you eaten?" — the same care, completely different grammar.

눈치 — READING THE ROOM

Koreans value what's not said as much as what is. 눈치 is the ability to read the room, sense tension, and respond appropriately without being told. It's trainable — and I teach it through situational quizzes.

Quiz: Your Korean friend says "괘찬아요" (It's okay). What do they really mean?

A) They're fine   B) They're upset but won't say it   C) Depends on context

Answer: C — and I'll teach you exactly how to read the difference.
