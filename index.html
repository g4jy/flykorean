Teaching Philosophy

Three Pillars of Korean Mastery

These aren't just teaching methods. They're the foundation of how Korean actually works — and why most textbooks get it wrong.

Pronunciation First

Most teachers start with grammar. I start with pronunciation. Even with just 10 words, if you pronounce them correctly, Koreans will understand you and be impressed. Korean is forgiving on grammar but unforgiving on sounds.

THE THREE CONSONANT LEVELS

English has nothing like this. This is why pronunciation matters so much.

가

Plain (예사소리)

Soft, relaxed — like "g" in "go" but gentler

카

Aspirated (거센소리)

Strong burst of air — hold your hand in front

까

Tensed (된소리)

Tight throat, no air — like the "k" in "sky"

Confuse these, and "coffee" becomes "nose bleed." Seriously.

Sentence Logic

Don't memorize patterns. Understand why Korean sentences work the way they do. English thinks "action first." Korean thinks "context first." Once you get this, you can create any sentence on your own.

WORD ORDER COMPARISON

Same sentence, two completely different ways of thinking.

English: Who → Action → What → Where

I bought a bag in Paris

Korean: Who → Where → What → Action

저는 파리에서 가방을 샤어요

Korean saves the verb for last — like building suspense in a story.

Cultural Context

Korean is deeply contextual. The same word can be polite, casual, or rude depending on the situation. Age, social status, closeness — all change how you speak. I teach you to read the room.

7 WAYS TO SAY "THANK YOU"

Use the wrong level, and you'll insult someone while trying to be polite.

고마워Close friends only
고마워요Casual polite
감사해요Polite
감사합니다Formal
감사드립니다Very formal / Business

I teach you when to use each one — and why Koreans can tell when you pick the wrong one.
