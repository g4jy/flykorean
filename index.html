Why I Teach Korean

Before you decide if I'm the right teacher for you, I want you to know who I am. Not a polished sales pitch — the real story. The parts I'm proud of and the parts that were hard. Because the way I teach today comes directly from everything below.

Chapter 1 — The Rise

I built something
from nothing.

Fresh out of college, I opened a self-directed learning coaching center in Korea. No investors, no connections, no safety net. Just a small office and a belief that teaching could be done better.

I built everything from scratch — tracking systems for every student, personalized study plans, follow-up protocols. Word spread. Within four years, I was ranked 170th out of 450,000 tutors on Korea's largest education platform. The top 0.03%.

Chapter 2 — The Price

Nobody tells you
what it costs.

Korean parents want results. Grades, rankings, certificates — in one of the most competitive education markets in the world, that's what they pay for. And I delivered.

But I also saw kids being crushed by the pressure. So I became more than a teacher. I became a connector — mediating between parents who wanted scores and students who needed someone to just listen. Counselor, study coach, translator between two generations who saw education completely differently.

I took less pay to spend more hours. I hired staff. Office rent, materials, custom curricula — all out of my own pocket. The work was meaningful. The math was not.

Chapter 3 — The Breaking Point

I remembered
too well.

I didn't burn out because I stopped caring. I burned out because I couldn't stop caring. Every student's struggle felt personal. Every parent's frustration landed on me. Every improvement felt like proof that if I just worked a little harder, I could fix the whole system.

I couldn't. My body broke before my motivation did. I closed the center. Packed a bag. Left Korea with no plan and no idea what came next.

Teaching Philosophy

Three Pillars of Korean Mastery

These aren't just teaching methods. They're the foundation of how Korean actually works — and why most textbooks get it wrong.

Most courses teach these separately. But in real Korean, they're inseparable. You can't fix pronunciation without understanding sound change rules (Pronunciation). Sound changes follow sentence patterns (Sentence Logic). And sentence patterns change completely based on who you're talking to (Cultural Context).

Sounds ↔ Logic ↔ Context

Sounds Before Grammar

Korean is forgiving on grammar but unforgiving on sounds. Even with just 10 words, correct pronunciation makes Koreans think you lived in Korea.

"Korean people care about pronunciation rather than grammar. If you sound right, they'll understand you."

Consonants: Three Levels of ㄱ/ㅋ/ㄲ

가

Plain (예사소리)

Soft, relaxed — like "g" in "go"

Minimal air release. Vocal cords relaxed.

가방 (bag) • 고기 (meat)

카

Aspirated (거센소리)

Strong burst of air — hold hand in front

Puff of air you can feel on your palm.

카페 (cafe) • 카드 (card)

까

Tensed (된소리)

Tight throat, no air released

Squeeze your throat, stop all airflow.

까피 wrong! 커피 = coffee

Vowels: Jaw & Lip Positions

아

Jaw: wide open

Lips: neutral

어

Jaw: half open

Lips: neutral

오

Jaw: half

Lips: rounded

우

Jaw: closed

Lips: pursed

으

Jaw: closed

Lips: spread wide

이

Jaw: closed

Lips: smile shape

Sound Changes: Written ≠ Spoken

맛있어요 → 마시셔요 "delicious"
한국어 → 한구거 "Korean language"

I teach you to lean into natural blending, not fight it.

Think Korean, Not Translate

English goes small→big. Korean goes big→small. Once you see the logic, you stop translating and start thinking in Korean.

"If I say something 주세요, they will give me something. Pattern over grammar."

WORD ORDER

Korean reverses English order — verb always last

ENDINGS & CONJUGATION

One root, many endings — tense, mood, formality

PARTICLES

Small markers that define who does what to whom

WORD ORDER: SHOPPING

English: Who → Action → What → Where

I bought a bag in Paris

Korean: Who → Where → What → Action

저는 파리에서 가방을 샤요

TENSE: JUST THE ENDING CHANGES

사요 → buy (present)
샬어요 → bought (past)
살 거예요 → will buy (future)

Learn one pattern → apply it to every verb.

NUANCE: THREE WAYS TO SAY "WILL DO"

할 거예요

Neutral plan — "I will do it"

할게요

Promise — "I'll do it (for you)"

하려고 해요

Intention — "I'm planning to do it"

Same "meaning" — completely different nuance.

BIG → SMALL: ADDRESSES & DATES

English: 42 Oak Street, Seoul, Korea

Korean: 한국, 서울, 오크 스트릿 42

English: February 13, 2026

Korean: 2026년 2월 13일

Three Cultural Pillars

Korean isn't just a language — it's a social operating system. The same sentence can be polite, casual, or insulting based on context. Understanding why Korean works this way is the key to speaking naturally, not just correctly.

"Korea and Japan are high contextual societies — less verbal, more non-verbal."

High Context Society

높은 맥락 문화

Why it matters: Korea's 5,000-year history as a densely populated, homogeneous society created a culture where people share unspoken context. Words are just the tip of the iceberg.

Context matters more than words. What's not said is as important as what is.

Quiz: Your friend says "괘찬아요". What do they mean?

A) Fine   B) Upset but won't say   C) Depends on context

Answer: C — I teach you to read the difference.

눈치 — reading social cues is a trainable skill.

Honorific System

높임말

Why it exists: Confucian values embedded hierarchy into the language itself. It's not optional politeness — it's grammatically mandatory. Using the wrong level is like speaking broken Korean.

The "Korean brain simulation" — instantly calculating which words to use:

Age → Relationship → Context → Formality Level
고마워Friends
감사해요Polite
감사합니다Formal
감사드립니다Very formal

밥 먹었어?

Group-Oriented Society

집단주의

Why it matters: Korea's agricultural roots required collective survival. This "we-first" thinking became baked into the language. You literally can't say "my country" naturally in Korean.

"We" comes before "I" in Korean thinking. Even grammar reflects this.

우리 나라

"Our country" (not "my country")

우리 엄마

"Our mom" (not "my mom")

우리 회사

"Our company" (not "my company")

Why Koreans ask your age: not nosiness — linguistic necessity for the group hierarchy.
